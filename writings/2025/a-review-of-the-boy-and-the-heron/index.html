A Review of The Boy and the Heron

March 16, 2025

This review serves as my final project for HNRS 280 (W'24). It contains spoilers for The Boy and the Heron. Please watch the movie if you haven't already. I promise it's worth it.

Vish

Standing in contrast to most Studio Ghibli films, the score of The Boy and the Heron spends much of its first hour either in ensembles of piano and chamber strings or complete silence. The film opens with its protagonist (Mahito) losing his mother in the firebombing of Tokyo. After a timeskip, his father is remarried. Mahito meets Natsuko, his father's new wife (and late mother's sister) as they evacuate to an estate in the countryside. The film is quiet. This introduction was not marked by a sweeping orchestral score, just the motif Ask Me Why:

Ask Me Why (Evacuation)

At this estate we are introduced to the eponymous gray heron. Its goal is to lure Mahito to a tower built by his grand-uncle. Its motif is two simple pitches forming a descending melodic 4th, and it plays each time the character is on screen:

idk bro

The heron attempts to convince Mahito that his mother is still alive, and in need of rescue. At first, Mahito resists this temptation and is angered by the heron's supposed insult. The heron's score picks up, as it points out to Mahito that he hasn't actually seen his mother's dead body.

idk bro

But Natsuko saves Mahito here, by firing an arrow at the heron. Mahito faints, and is taken to his bedroom. At its surface, then, The Boy and the Heron is about a boy learning to accept his mother's death and father's remarriage. Though Mahito is distant to Natsuko at the beginning of the film, they eventually grow to be on better terms. While there, Mahito finds a book left for him by his mother titled How Do You Live?. As he begins to cry, the film's full score begins to play— though it's the same motif as before:

Ask Me Why (Mother's Message)

This is interrupted when Mahito learns that Natsuko herself has been lured by the heron to the tower. He takes his makeshift bow and arrow built specifically to confront the heron, and makes his way to the tower. At its entrance there reads an inscription; “fecimi la divina potestate”, an Italian phrase that translates to “divine power made me” in English. (Given that later it is revealed the tower was built around a meteor that fell from the sky, I think this is apt!) It comes from Dante Alighieri's Inferno, written on the gates of hell.

Through me one goes into the city of sorrow,
through me one goes into the city of eternal woe,
though me one goes among the lost people.

Justice moved my high Maker;
divine power made me,
supreme wisdom and primal love.

Inferno, Canto III
