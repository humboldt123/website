Hands, Copyright, and the Future

March 4, 2026

This essay was originally written for my friend Brooke. Hi Brooke.

Vish

A friend recently asked me how generative AI creates “art” if not by scraping (and training on) material that already exists. Simply put, it would not be able to. But this, I think, glosses over a much more interesting truth. Attached to their message was a screenshot in which they said that they were a “REAL artist” and that “generative ai capitalizes off the work [they] and other artist[s] put so much time into.” I think the implication this carries is well worth addressing.

As a disclaimer, I am not a huge fan of much of the ‘art’ produced by generative AI models. It comes across as bland and uninspired—and I associate it with being “cheap.” That many of its early advocates seem not to have any taste—pairing canned looking AI-generated images with canned AI-generated writing on maybe the least thoughtful subjects ever for AI generated blogs—doesn’t help. I have the same reflexive reaction to images that feel AI generated as I do those Ape NFTs from a few years back. This, I think, is a common opinion.

At the same time, many of the arguments that artists put forth against it strike me as remarkably confused. I’ve heard generative AI “sucks,” that it constitutes some kind of “theft,” lacks some innate quality of the human soul barring it from being truly “artistic,” and that—all told—its adoption ought to be halted as it would be bad for artists.

It Sucks

I think the first argument is easiest to dismiss outright, but it is still worth going over. When diffusion models started to rapidly improve in 2022, they struggled to articulate certain features. Hands, in particular. You might remember gems like these. Artists would mock the technology for being unable to do something humans can do:

artist: it's funny how ai cant draw hands

techbro: lmao?? why, can y--

artist: yes.

@lou_quorice (x.com)

It took a couple months for AI to crack hands, then the line of argument retreated to being able to capture more acute details or some subtleties of lighting. Given a month or so, frontier models would crack those as well. In principle, there is nothing that the technology will not advance beyond given enough time. Claims that it sucks really bother me because they’re just cope. That’s all they are. They constitute a flat-out denial of reality. In truth, this technology is only ever going to improve. And denying that serves nobody. I think this Substack piece does this point more justice than I could. Though about language models, the argument it makes applies to diffusion models just as well. Theft & Copyright

Molly Crabapple, a contributing editor for VICE and a New York based artist and author, recently appeared on a 60 Minutes segment in which she lambasted AI as the “greatest art heist in history.” She claimed that “they [tech, presumably?] stole billions and billions of images (emph. added)” in order to train their models.

She claimed that her work had been stolen, the evidence of this was the similarity a sketch she made of Aleppo, Syria had with an image generated by an AI prompted to sketch Aleppo.

This is ludicrous. Maybe they look the same because they're both renditions of the same Aleppo. Does Crabapple think she owns how the buildings look?
